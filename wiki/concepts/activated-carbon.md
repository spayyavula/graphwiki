# Activated Carbon

**Definition:** A highly porous carbon material engineered for very large surface area and strong adsorption capacity, widely used in purification, separations, catalysis, and catalyst-support applications.

**Also known as:** Activated charcoal, porous activated carbon, high-surface-area carbon.

## Overview
[coverage: medium]

Activated carbon matters because it is one of the clearest examples of carbon engineered for function rather than simply identified by allotrope. Its importance comes from porosity, surface area, and surface chemistry. Those features make it central to adsorption and purification technologies, but they also place it directly inside the carbon-catalysis branch because many catalytic systems depend on activated carbon as either an active carbonaceous material or a carefully designed support.

This topic is useful in GraphWiki because it sharpens the distinction between carbon as structure and carbon as interface. Activated carbon often appears where transport, adsorption, and surface reactions matter at the same time. In some settings it mainly provides high surface area and mass-transfer pathways for metal or oxide catalysts. In others, its own defect structure, doping state, or functional groups contribute directly to catalytic behavior. That is why it sits naturally beside [[Carbon Catalysis]] and the comparison [[Carbon Supports vs True Carbon Catalysts]].

Activated carbon also helps connect Carbon Science to industrial practice. It belongs not only to nanoscience or advanced device research but to water treatment, gas cleanup, separations, catalytic beds, and broader process engineering. That practical breadth makes it a strong bridge between carbon materials, surface chemistry, and applied materials science.

## Key Properties

- Very high internal surface area created through activation and pore development
- Strong adsorption behavior for gases, solutes, and organic contaminants
- Surface chemistry can be tuned through oxidation, functionalization, or doping
- Often used as a catalyst support, but can also participate directly in catalysis
- Connects carbon materials to filtration, purification, reaction engineering, and environmental systems

## Examples

- Activated carbon is used in purification systems because its pore network captures contaminants efficiently
- Metal or oxide catalysts are often dispersed on activated carbon to exploit its large surface area and transport pathways
- Functionalized activated carbons can contribute directly to catalytic or electrochemical performance rather than serving only as inert supports
