# Carbon Capture and Storage

**Definition:** A set of technologies and processes designed to capture carbon dioxide emissions from industrial or energy systems and store them so they do not enter the atmosphere.

**Also known as:** CCS, carbon sequestration, carbon capture and sequestration.

## Overview
[coverage: medium]

Carbon capture and storage is one of the major intervention strategies in carbon science. Instead of focusing only on reducing emissions at the source, CCS attempts to separate CO2 from flue gas or process streams, transport it, and store it in geological formations or other long-term reservoirs. Its significance comes from sectors where emissions are difficult to eliminate completely, such as cement, steel, and some large-scale energy systems.

CCS is closely tied to the [[Carbon Cycle]] because it is ultimately an attempt to redirect carbon flows away from the atmosphere and toward more stable storage pathways. That means CCS is not just an engineering problem but also a systems problem involving storage permanence, monitoring, leakage risk, cost, and policy coordination. The scientific challenge is not only whether carbon can be captured, but whether it can be stored at the scale, safety level, and cost needed for climate relevance.

For GraphWiki, CCS is a useful bridge concept because it connects scientific processes, industrial infrastructure, and policy framing. It can therefore anchor future pages on sequestration, industrial decarbonization, and carbon accounting.

## Key Properties

- Involves capture, transport, storage, and long-term monitoring stages
- Often targets point-source emissions from industrial or power-generation systems
- Raises questions of cost, scale, permanence, and leakage risk
- Connects engineering, climate policy, and earth-science storage models
- Often evaluated as part of broader decarbonization portfolios rather than as a standalone solution
