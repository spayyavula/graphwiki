# Coal

**Definition:** A carbon-rich sedimentary fuel formed from ancient plant matter and widely used in electricity generation, industrial heat, and metallurgical processing.

**Also known as:** Fossil coal, coal fuel, carbonaceous fossil fuel.

## Overview
[coverage: medium]

Coal belongs in Carbon Science because it is one of the clearest cases where long-stored geologic carbon is pulled into modern human systems. It is not just an energy commodity. It is a pathway by which ancient biomass, transformed over millions of years, becomes electricity, heat, metallurgical coke, and large-scale CO2 emissions. That makes coal a bridge between the carbon cycle, industrial energy systems, and carbon-management efforts such as [[Carbon Capture and Storage]].

The topic also matters because coal shows that carbon science is not limited to advanced materials or climate modeling. Carbon appears here as a fuel with ranks, heating values, impurities, and strong environmental consequences. Coal therefore gives the Carbon Science branch an industrial and energy-systems wing that complements the existing materials wing built around [[Carbon Allotrope]], [[Carbon Catalysis]], and [[Carbon-Carbon Composites]].

## Key Properties

- Formed from ancient biomass under heat and pressure over geologic time
- Classified by rank, including anthracite, bituminous, subbituminous, and lignite
- Carbon content and heating value vary strongly across ranks
- Used in electricity generation, industrial heat, coke production, and some gasification pathways
- Connects fossil-carbon storage directly to emissions, air-pollution control, and decarbonization strategy

## Examples

- Coal-fired power plants burn coal to raise steam and generate electricity
- Metallurgical coal is converted to coke for iron and steel production
- Coal combustion is a major case where carbon-management strategies such as capture and storage become relevant
