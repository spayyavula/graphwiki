# Gibbs Free Energy

**Definition:** A thermodynamic potential, usually written as $G$, that helps determine whether a process is favored at constant temperature and pressure.

**Also known as:** Gibbs energy, free energy in many chemistry contexts, Gibbs potential.

## Overview
[coverage: medium]

Gibbs free energy is one of the most important concepts in chemical thermodynamics because it connects energy, entropy, and equilibrium into a single criterion that is directly useful for real chemical systems. In constant-temperature, constant-pressure settings, a decrease in Gibbs free energy indicates that a process is thermodynamically favored. That is why it appears everywhere in chemistry-heavy thermodynamics: reaction spontaneity, phase stability, solution behavior, electrochemistry, and multicomponent equilibrium.

The concept matters because thermodynamics is rarely used in practice as a loose discussion of heat and work alone. Chemists and chemical engineers usually need a criterion for whether a reaction proceeds, where equilibrium lies, and how changes in temperature, pressure, or composition alter that equilibrium. Gibbs free energy provides that criterion. It is closely linked to [[Equilibrium Constant|equilibrium constants]], [[Chemical Potential]], and phase coexistence.

For GraphWiki, [[Gibbs Free Energy]] makes the equilibrium logic of [[Chemical Thermodynamics]] more explicit. It shows how [[Entropy]] and enthalpy-style energy contributions combine into a practical decision quantity for reactions and materials systems, which is why it links naturally to [[Thermodynamics]], [[Chemical Thermodynamics]], [[Chemical Potential]], and [[Phase Transition]].

## Key Properties

- Central criterion for spontaneity at constant temperature and pressure
- Combines energetic and entropic contributions into one thermodynamic potential
- Closely tied to reaction equilibrium and equilibrium constants
- Useful for phase stability, electrochemistry, solution behavior, and materials thermodynamics
- Often introduced as the most practical free-energy quantity in chemistry courses

## Relationships

- **Is-a:** Thermodynamic potential, core chemical-thermodynamics concept
- **Has-part:** Standard free-energy change, reaction free-energy change, equilibrium relation, composition dependence
- **Related:** [[Thermodynamics]], [[Chemical Thermodynamics]], [[Entropy]], [[Chemical Potential]], [[Phase Transition]]
- **Used-by:** Reaction-equilibrium analysis, electrochemistry, solution thermodynamics, materials phase-stability analysis

## Examples

- Using the sign of $\Delta G$ to judge whether a reaction is favored under specified conditions
- Relating standard Gibbs free-energy change to an equilibrium constant
- Comparing competing phases in a material system to determine which is more stable
