# Carbon Allotrope

**Definition:** A structurally distinct form of elemental carbon whose properties depend on how carbon atoms are arranged and bonded.

**Also known as:** Carbon form, allotrope of carbon.

## Overview
[coverage: medium]

Carbon is unusual in how many structurally different forms it can take. Diamond, graphite, graphene, fullerenes, and carbon nanotubes are all made of the same element, but their atomic arrangements produce radically different electrical, mechanical, and thermal behavior. This makes carbon allotropes a central concept in the materials side of carbon science.

The topic matters because it connects atomic structure to macroscopic function. Graphite conducts electricity and lubricates; diamond is extremely hard and thermally conductive; graphene and carbon nanotubes have become central in nanoscience and advanced materials research. [[Graphene in Semiconductors]] makes that semiconductor-facing role explicit by showing why graphene matters in electronic materials even though pristine graphene is not a conventional band-gap semiconductor. [[Fullerenes]] extend the picture further by showing how carbon can also form closed molecular cages with distinctive curvature-driven behavior. Carbon science therefore does not only mean carbon fluxes in the climate system. It also includes the chemistry and physics of carbon-based materials.

For GraphWiki, carbon allotropes provide the cleanest bridge between the Carbon Science topic and [[Materials Science Academic Source Guide]]. They allow the topic to connect climate-facing carbon concepts with structure-property reasoning in materials research. They also open naturally into more application-facing materials such as [[Carbon-Carbon Composites]], where carbon form and carbon processing feed into real engineering systems, and into [[Carbon Catalysis]], where carbon structure and electronic tuning create catalytically active materials.

## Key Properties

- Different allotropes arise from different bonding and geometric arrangements
- Properties vary dramatically across hardness, conductivity, strength, and dimensionality
- Includes well-known forms such as diamond, graphite, graphene, fullerenes, and nanotubes
- Important in nanoscience, electronics, structural materials, and energy applications
- Connects atomic-scale structure to bulk material behavior
- Provides the materials-side foundation for more applied carbon systems such as C/C composites
- Supports catalysis research because edge structure, defects, and dopants in carbon forms can generate active chemical sites

## Examples

- Diamond's three-dimensional bonding leads to exceptional hardness
- Graphene is a single atomic layer of carbon in a hexagonal lattice
- Graphene is also central to semiconductor research because its transport properties and tunable interfaces make it valuable in electronic heterostructures
- Carbon nanotubes combine low weight with high strength and unusual electronic properties
- Fullerene cages show how carbon curvature can create molecular-scale allotropes with distinct chemical behavior
- Doped graphene and nanotube systems are also widely studied as catalytic materials for oxygen reduction and CO2 conversion
