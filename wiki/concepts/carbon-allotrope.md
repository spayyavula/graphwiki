# Carbon Allotrope

**Definition:** A structurally distinct form of elemental carbon whose properties depend on how carbon atoms are arranged and bonded.

**Also known as:** Carbon form, allotrope of carbon.

## Overview
[coverage: medium]

Carbon is unusual in how many structurally different forms it can take. Diamond, graphite, graphene, fullerenes, and carbon nanotubes are all made of the same element, but their atomic arrangements produce radically different electrical, mechanical, and thermal behavior. This makes carbon allotropes a central concept in the materials side of carbon science.

The topic matters because it connects atomic structure to macroscopic function. Graphite conducts electricity and lubricates; diamond is extremely hard and thermally conductive; graphene and carbon nanotubes have become central in nanoscience and advanced materials research. Carbon science therefore does not only mean carbon fluxes in the climate system. It also includes the chemistry and physics of carbon-based materials.

For GraphWiki, carbon allotropes provide the cleanest bridge between the Carbon Science topic and [[Materials Science Academic Source Guide]]. They allow the topic to connect climate-facing carbon concepts with structure-property reasoning in materials research.

## Key Properties

- Different allotropes arise from different bonding and geometric arrangements
- Properties vary dramatically across hardness, conductivity, strength, and dimensionality
- Includes well-known forms such as diamond, graphite, graphene, fullerenes, and nanotubes
- Important in nanoscience, electronics, structural materials, and energy applications
- Connects atomic-scale structure to bulk material behavior
