# Graphene in Semiconductors

**Definition:** The role of graphene in semiconductor science and devices, including its use as a high-mobility electronic material, an interfacial transport layer, and an engineered platform whose electronic structure can sometimes be tuned toward semiconducting behavior.

**Also known as:** Graphene-semiconductor systems, semiconducting graphene, graphene semiconductor heterostructures.

## Overview
[coverage: medium]

Graphene belongs in this wiki not only as a carbon allotrope but also as a semiconductor-facing material. That distinction matters because graphene sits in an unusual position: pristine graphene is not a conventional semiconductor with an easy transistor-ready band gap, yet it remains one of the most important materials in semiconductor research because of its exceptional carrier mobility, conductivity, atomically thin form, and interface behavior.

This is why graphene appears so often in semiconductor discussions despite the famous band-gap problem. In its ideal form, graphene is usually described as a zero-bandgap semimetal. That makes digital on-off switching difficult. But the same electronic structure also makes graphene attractive for high-speed transport, contact engineering, sensing, optoelectronics, and hybrid device design. Researchers therefore do not treat graphene as a simple silicon replacement. They treat it as a platform whose electronic role depends on how it is integrated, patterned, doped, stacked, or grown on specific substrates.

For GraphWiki, this page is useful because it closes the loop between [[Carbon Allotrope]] and [[Materials Science]]. It shows how a carbon material can matter directly in semiconductor research, not only in catalysis or composites. It also gives a cleaner place to discuss heterostructures, band engineering, and the current difference between pristine graphene, engineered semiconducting graphene, and graphene-semiconductor hybrid devices.

## Key Properties

- Pristine graphene is typically a zero-bandgap semimetal rather than a conventional semiconductor
- High carrier mobility and conductivity make it attractive for semiconductor-adjacent devices and interfaces
- Band-gap engineering is a central challenge in graphene electronics
- Graphene often appears in semiconductor heterostructures as a transport layer, Schottky-contact component, or photosensitizer
- The topic connects carbon nanomaterials with electronic materials, optoelectronics, and device physics

## Examples

- Graphene-semiconductor Schottky and Type II heterostructures are used to improve charge separation and transport
- Reduced graphene oxide is coupled with semiconductor materials to improve photocatalytic and interfacial electronic behavior
- Epitaxial graphene on silicon carbide is studied as a route toward functional graphene semiconductors with transistor-relevant switching properties
