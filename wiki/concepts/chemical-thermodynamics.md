# Chemical Thermodynamics

**Definition:** The part of thermodynamics that studies energy, entropy, free energy, equilibrium, and chemical potential in reacting and multicomponent systems.

**Also known as:** Reaction thermodynamics, equilibrium thermodynamics in chemistry, thermodynamics of chemical systems.

## Overview
[coverage: medium]

Chemical thermodynamics focuses on the thermodynamic logic of substances, mixtures, reactions, and phase equilibria. Instead of stopping at general statements about heat and work, it asks the chemistry-facing questions: when is a reaction spontaneous, what equilibrium composition is favored, how do temperature and pressure shift equilibrium, and how do free energy, [[Equilibrium Constant]], and chemical potential govern real chemical systems.

This is one of the most common ways thermodynamics is taught in chemistry departments and chemical-engineering contexts. The subject centers on [[Gibbs Free Energy]], enthalpy, entropy, [[Equilibrium Constant|equilibrium constants]], activities, partial molar quantities, and [[Chemical Potential]]. Those ideas are what let thermodynamics explain acid-base equilibria, combustion products, battery reactions, solution behavior, and phase coexistence in mixtures.

For GraphWiki, [[Chemical Thermodynamics]] makes the chemistry layer of the Thermodynamics branch explicit. It helps explain why [[Thermodynamics]] is not only about engines or abstract state variables. It is also the language for chemical equilibrium, reaction direction, phase stability, and multicomponent systems, which is why it links naturally to [[Entropy]], [[Kinetics]], [[Phase Diagram]], [[Materials Science]], and combustion-oriented source material.

## Key Properties

- Focuses on reacting systems, mixtures, solutions, and phase equilibria
- Uses free-energy reasoning to determine spontaneity and equilibrium conditions
- Treats [[Chemical Potential]] as a central quantity for multicomponent systems
- Connects thermodynamic state functions to [[Equilibrium Constant|equilibrium constants]] and reaction direction
- Commonly taught through combustion, electrochemistry, solution chemistry, and materials examples

## Relationships

- **Is-a:** Chemistry-facing branch of thermodynamics
- **Has-part:** [[Gibbs Free Energy]], enthalpy, [[Chemical Potential]], [[Equilibrium Constant|equilibrium constants]], activities, partial molar quantities
- **Related:** [[Thermodynamics]], [[Entropy]], [[Kinetics]], [[Phase Diagram]], [[Phase Transition]], [[Materials Science]]
- **Used-by:** Physical chemistry, chemical engineering, combustion analysis, electrochemistry, materials thermodynamics

## Examples

- Determining whether a reaction is spontaneous from the sign of a Gibbs free-energy change
- Relating an equilibrium constant to temperature and reaction energetics
- Using chemical potential to explain phase coexistence or species transfer in a multicomponent system
