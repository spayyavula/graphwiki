# Fullerenes

**Definition:** A family of closed-cage carbon molecules, including the well-known C60 buckminsterfullerene, in which carbon atoms form curved hollow structures rather than flat sheets or extended three-dimensional networks.

**Also known as:** Fullerene molecules, buckyballs, cage-like carbon allotropes.

## Overview
[coverage: medium]

Fullerenes make the Carbon Science branch more complete because they show that carbon allotropy is not limited to graphite, diamond, graphene, and nanotubes. Their significance lies in the way curvature changes carbon chemistry and physics. Instead of flat sp2 sheets, fullerene structures wrap into closed cages, producing distinctive electronic, optical, and reactivity patterns. That makes them a natural bridge from introductory allotrope discussions into nanoscience, molecular materials, and carbon-based functional chemistry.

The requested fullerene literature thread is also important for balance. Carbon materials discussions often collapse toward graphene and nanotubes because those are easy to connect to electronics and mechanics. A dedicated fullerene page corrects that bias. It lets GraphWiki represent the fullerene family as a real node inside the carbon-materials branch rather than just a word inside [[Carbon Allotrope]].

Within the repository, fullerenes are also useful because they connect chemistry and materials viewpoints. They can be discussed as molecular allotropes, but also as precursors for derivatives, supramolecular assemblies, and advanced materials applications. That gives the Carbon Science branch a richer materials-science identity alongside the existing climate and carbon-management material.

## Key Properties

- Consist of curved carbon cages rather than flat sheets or extended 3D frameworks
- Include archetypal species such as C60 and larger cage structures
- Exhibit distinctive electronic, optical, and redox behavior compared with graphite or graphene
- Sit naturally at the interface of carbon chemistry, nanoscience, and materials science
- Help complete the allotrope branch so it includes molecular as well as extended carbon forms

- Functionalized fullerene derivatives are studied for electronic and molecular-materials applications
- Fullerene discussions help explain how curvature changes the behavior of sp2-bonded carbon networks
