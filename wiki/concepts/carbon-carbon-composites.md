# Carbon-Carbon Composites

**Definition:** Composite materials in which carbon fibers are reinforced by a carbon matrix, producing lightweight thermo-structural materials with strong high-temperature, friction, and thermal-shock performance.

**Also known as:** C/C composites, carbon composites for high-temperature service, carbon-fiber-reinforced carbon.

## Overview
[coverage: medium]

Carbon-carbon composites are the clearest way to extend the Carbon Science branch from carbon forms into carbon engineering. They are not just another allotrope. They are manufactured materials whose performance depends on fiber architecture, matrix processing, densification route, porosity control, oxidation protection, and application-specific tribology. That makes them a strong bridge between [[Carbon Allotrope]], [[Materials Science]], and application-oriented aerospace engineering.

They are especially important because of aircraft brakes. Carbon-carbon brake discs became a canonical application precisely because they combine low weight with high heat absorption, high-temperature friction performance, and strong dimensional stability under extreme braking loads. This application gives the Carbon Science branch a concrete engineering destination: carbon materials are not only scientifically interesting, they are deployed in severe thermal and frictional environments.

The materials challenge is that the same properties that make C/C composites attractive also create design complexity. Microstructure, reinforcement layout, oxidation resistance, and coatings matter directly to lifetime and performance. That makes the topic a good fit for GraphWiki's broader materials-science direction and gives Carbon Science a more mature engineering wing.

## Key Properties

- Combine carbon-fiber reinforcement with a carbon matrix rather than a polymer or metal matrix
- Retain useful mechanical and frictional performance at very high temperatures
- Offer low density, strong thermal shock resistance, and high heat absorption capacity
- Are strongly shaped by densification route, porosity, fiber architecture, and oxidation protection
- Have major aerospace use cases, especially aircraft brake systems and thermal-protection components

## Examples

- Aircraft brake discs use carbon-carbon composites because they tolerate repeated high-energy braking with strong thermal stability
- Thermal-protection components use C/C composites where ablation resistance and low mass both matter
- Oxidation-protective coatings are added when service environments would otherwise degrade the carbon matrix rapidly
