# Carbon Catalysis

**Definition:** The use of carbon-based materials as catalytically active media, where the carbon framework itself, or carefully engineered active sites within it, promotes chemical reactions rather than serving only as a support.

**Also known as:** Carbon catalysts, carbon-based catalysis, metal-free carbon catalysis, carbon electrocatalysis.

## Overview
[coverage: medium]

Carbon belongs in this branch not only because it forms important materials, but because it can also function as an effective catalyst family. That matters because it changes the meaning of Carbon Science. The topic is not limited to carbon cycles, sequestration, and structural materials. It also includes chemically active carbon frameworks whose electronic structure can be tuned to accelerate reactions and steer selectivity.

Modern carbon catalysis is closely tied to defect engineering, heteroatom doping, and nanostructure design. Perfect graphitic carbon is often relatively inert, but once the local electronic symmetry is broken, carbon can host catalytically active centers. Nitrogen-, boron-, sulfur-, and phosphorus-doped carbons, defect-rich graphene, carbon nanotubes, [[Activated Carbon]], porous carbons, and carbon-supported hybrids are all examples of how the carbon framework can become an active participant in catalysis rather than a passive scaffold.

This branch is especially important because it connects carbon materials to energy and environmental chemistry. Current literature treats carbon catalysts as relevant to oxygen reduction, CO2 conversion, hydrogen peroxide generation, fuel-cell electrocatalysis, and broader green-chemistry design. It also forces a useful distinction between carbon as support and carbon as a true catalytic medium, which is captured explicitly in [[Carbon Supports vs True Carbon Catalysts]]. That gives the Carbon Science branch a strong application-facing pathway alongside [[Carbon-Carbon Composites]] and a chemistry-facing pathway alongside [[Fullerenes]] and [[Carbon Allotrope]].

## Key Properties

- Catalytic activity can emerge from heteroatom doping, defects, edge sites, porosity, and charge redistribution in carbon frameworks
- Carbon can function as a metal-free catalyst, a doped catalytic material, or part of a metal-carbon hybrid catalyst
- Activity and selectivity depend strongly on local electronic structure and active-site architecture
- Carbon catalysis is important in electrocatalysis, especially oxygen reduction and CO2 conversion
- The field links carbon nanomaterials, surface chemistry, energy systems, and green chemical synthesis
- Includes both high-surface-area support roles and genuinely active carbon frameworks

## Examples

- Nitrogen-doped carbon nanotubes and graphene-derived materials are studied as effective oxygen-reduction catalysts in fuel cells
- Defect-rich and heteroatom-doped carbon frameworks can promote CO2 conversion and influence product selectivity
- Carbon-supported metal or oxide systems use metal-carbon interfacial effects to change catalytic behavior relative to the unsupported metal alone
- Activated carbon often appears at the boundary between support and active material because its surface chemistry can be tuned for adsorption and reaction roles
