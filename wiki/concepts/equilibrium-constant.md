# Equilibrium Constant

**Definition:** A quantity that expresses the equilibrium position of a reaction in terms of the activities or effective concentrations of reactants and products.

**Also known as:** Equilibrium coefficient, reaction equilibrium constant, $K$.

## Overview
[coverage: medium]

The equilibrium constant is one of the most recognizable outputs of chemical thermodynamics. It tells us how a reaction is balanced at equilibrium and provides a compact way to connect thermodynamic favorability with observable chemical composition. Instead of speaking only in qualitative terms about whether a reaction is favored, the equilibrium constant gives a quantitative description of how strongly products or reactants are favored under specified conditions.

This concept matters because it is the bridge between abstract free-energy reasoning and practical chemistry. In chemistry courses, thermodynamics often becomes concrete when students learn how Gibbs free energy relates to $K$, how temperature shifts equilibrium, and how reaction quotients compare with equilibrium values. That is why equilibrium constants appear across acid-base chemistry, combustion, electrochemistry, atmospheric chemistry, and materials processing.

For GraphWiki, [[Equilibrium Constant]] makes the chemistry-facing branch of thermodynamics more complete. It links [[Gibbs Free Energy]] and [[Chemical Thermodynamics]] to the language used in real reacting systems, while also connecting to [[Chemical Potential]] and the broader equilibrium logic of [[Thermodynamics]].

## Key Properties

- Quantifies the equilibrium composition of a reaction system
- Depends on temperature and the thermodynamic standard state
- Closely linked to standard Gibbs free-energy change
- Distinct from the reaction quotient, which describes nonequilibrium composition
- Widely used in chemistry, combustion, solution thermodynamics, and materials processing

## Relationships

- **Is-a:** Core chemical-thermodynamics quantity
- **Has-part:** Standard-state relation, temperature dependence, activity-based expression, reaction quotient comparison
- **Related:** [[Chemical Thermodynamics]], [[Gibbs Free Energy]], [[Chemical Potential]], [[Thermodynamics]]
- **Used-by:** Physical chemistry, combustion analysis, electrochemistry, equilibrium modeling, reaction-system design

## Examples

- Using a large $K$ value to show that products are strongly favored at equilibrium
- Comparing a reaction quotient with $K$ to determine which way a system will shift
- Relating a change in temperature to a change in equilibrium position for a reaction
