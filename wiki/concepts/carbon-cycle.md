# Carbon Cycle

**Definition:** The set of processes through which carbon moves among the atmosphere, oceans, biosphere, soils, and geologic reservoirs over timescales ranging from days to millions of years.

**Also known as:** Global carbon cycle, biogeochemical carbon cycle.

## Overview
[coverage: medium]

The carbon cycle is the core systems concept behind much of carbon science. It describes how carbon is stored in different reservoirs and how it moves between them through processes such as photosynthesis, respiration, decomposition, ocean exchange, weathering, and combustion. Understanding those flows is essential for explaining both the natural regulation of Earth's climate and the scale of human disruption caused by fossil-fuel use and land-use change.

The carbon cycle matters because climate change is not only about emissions in isolation, but about how emissions alter the balance among sources, sinks, and long-lived storage. Atmospheric CO2 accumulates when emissions outpace the combined uptake capacity of oceans, vegetation, and soils. This makes the carbon cycle a bridge concept connecting climate science, ecosystem science, geoscience, and carbon management.

For GraphWiki, the carbon cycle is also a strong graph domain. Reservoirs, fluxes, feedbacks, and interventions can be modeled as connected entities and relationships rather than isolated facts. ## Key Properties

- Includes major reservoirs such as atmosphere, ocean, vegetation, soils, and geologic carbon stores
- Includes fluxes such as photosynthesis, respiration, ocean uptake, decomposition, and combustion
- Operates across short biological timescales and long geological timescales
- Strongly affected by anthropogenic emissions and land-use change
- Central to climate modeling and carbon-management policy

## Examples

- Photosynthesis transfers atmospheric carbon into biomass
- Ocean uptake absorbs part of anthropogenic CO2 emissions but changes ocean chemistry
- Fossil-fuel combustion moves long-stored geologic carbon into the atmosphere on human timescales
