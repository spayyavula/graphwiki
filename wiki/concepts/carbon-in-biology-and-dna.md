# Carbon in Biology and DNA

**Definition:** The role of carbon as the structural basis of biological molecules, with DNA providing a clear example of carbon-centered molecular architecture in living systems.

**Also known as:** Biological carbon chemistry, carbon in biomolecules, carbon and DNA.

## Overview
[coverage: medium]

Carbon belongs in biology because its bonding flexibility makes stable, information-rich, and functionally diverse molecules possible. Within this branch, DNA is the clearest anchor example. DNA is not only a genetics term; it is a carbon-containing molecule whose sugar backbone and bases depend on carbon chemistry. That makes DNA a useful reminder that Carbon Science is not limited to climate systems, fuels, or advanced materials.

This page gives the Carbon Science topic a biology-facing extension without turning the repository into a full genomics wiki. The goal is narrower and more useful: to show that carbon is foundational to molecular life in the same way that it is foundational to fuels, catalysts, and engineered materials. Adding this node makes the carbon branch intellectually broader and helps explain why carbon is central across earth systems, industry, materials, and biology. The adjacent page [[DNA Discovery]] adds the history-of-discovery angle so the branch covers not only molecular composition but also why DNA structure became scientifically decisive.

## Key Properties

- Carbon forms the backbone of biologically important molecular frameworks
- DNA is built from carbon-containing sugars and bases arranged into an information-carrying polymer
- Biological carbon chemistry depends on stable covalent bonding and structural diversity
- Extends Carbon Science toward biochemistry without requiring a separate full biology topic branch
- Provides a learner-facing bridge from carbon as material to carbon as molecular basis of life

## Examples

- DNA stores biological information in sequences built on a carbon-containing sugar-phosphate backbone
- The deoxyribose component of DNA is a direct example of carbon embedded in biological structure
- Carbon science therefore reaches into life science as well as into fuels, climate, and materials
