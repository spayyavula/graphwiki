# Chemical Potential

**Definition:** The thermodynamic quantity that measures how the Gibbs free energy of a system changes when the amount of a component changes, holding the other relevant variables fixed.

**Also known as:** Partial molar Gibbs free energy, species potential in thermodynamic context.

## Overview
[coverage: medium]

Chemical potential is one of the core ideas that makes chemical thermodynamics work for mixtures, reactions, and phase equilibria. It tells us how strongly a species contributes to the system's thermodynamic state and provides the language for equilibrium across phases and components. In multicomponent systems, it plays the role that pressure or temperature plays in simpler systems: it is the quantity that must balance appropriately for equilibrium to hold.

The concept is especially important because many chemistry-facing thermodynamics problems are not about a single pure substance. They are about reacting mixtures, solutions, gases, alloys, or systems with multiple coexisting phases. In those settings, chemical potential explains diffusion tendencies, reaction driving force, phase partitioning, and species transfer. It is tightly connected to activities, fugacity-style reasoning, and Gibbs free energy.

For GraphWiki, [[Chemical Potential]] helps prevent the Thermodynamics branch from staying too qualitative. It makes the multicomponent and equilibrium side of [[Chemical Thermodynamics]] explicit, linking reaction spontaneity and free-energy reasoning to phase coexistence, mixtures, and materials systems. That is why it naturally connects to [[Gibbs Free Energy]], [[Equilibrium Constant]], [[Phase Diagram]], [[Chemical Thermodynamics]], and [[Materials Science]].

## Key Properties

- Governs equilibrium and transfer tendencies in multicomponent systems
- Can be interpreted as partial molar Gibbs free energy
- Central to mixture thermodynamics, phase equilibrium, and reaction equilibrium
- Connects activities, composition dependence, and free-energy minimization
- Important in chemistry, chemical engineering, electrochemistry, and materials thermodynamics

- Explaining why a species moves from one phase to another until equilibrium is reached

- Describing how composition changes alter thermodynamic driving force in an alloy or solution
