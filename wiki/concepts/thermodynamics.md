# Thermodynamics

**Definition:** The branch of science that studies energy, heat, work, entropy, and equilibrium, especially how these quantities constrain the direction and possibility of physical and chemical processes.

**Also known as:** Equilibrium thermodynamics, classical thermodynamics, thermal science foundation.

## Overview
[coverage: medium]

Thermodynamics is one of the central organizing frameworks of modern science and engineering. It explains which processes can happen, which direction they tend to proceed in, and what energy transformations are possible under given conditions. Rather than tracking the detailed microscopic motion of every particle, thermodynamics works with macroscopic state variables such as temperature, pressure, energy, and entropy to describe the behavior of systems in aggregate.

The subject matters because it provides a common language across chemistry, physics, engineering, materials science, and increasingly even computation. Questions about heat engines, chemical equilibrium, phase stability, combustion, batteries, and information-processing systems all depend on thermodynamic reasoning. For GraphWiki, thermodynamics is also a strong bridge topic because it connects directly to [[Chemical Thermodynamics]], [[Materials Science]], [[Phase Diagram]] reasoning, and the systems-level logic behind [[Carbon Cycle]] and [[Carbon Capture and Storage]].

In practice, thermodynamics is often taught as a balance between state functions and process constraints. Energy conservation explains what must be accounted for, while entropy and free-energy ideas help explain what changes are favored. In chemistry-heavy settings, that usually means emphasizing [[Gibbs Free Energy]], [[Equilibrium Constant|equilibrium]], [[Chemical Potential]], and reaction direction in multicomponent systems. This makes [[Entropy]] and [[Chemical Thermodynamics]] two of the most important related concepts, especially when moving from introductory thermodynamics to spontaneous processes, irreversibility, and equilibrium.

## Chemical-Thermodynamics Angle
[coverage: medium]

One major teaching angle for thermodynamics is explicitly chemical. In that framing, the important questions are not only how energy is conserved, but how reactions, mixtures, and phases behave at equilibrium. [[Gibbs Free Energy]], [[Equilibrium Constant|equilibrium constants]], activities, fugacity, enthalpy changes, and [[Chemical Potential]] become the main explanatory tools.

That is why many learners first encounter thermodynamics in physical chemistry, chemical engineering, combustion, or battery science rather than in a purely mechanical or engine-based setting. For GraphWiki, this means the Thermodynamics branch should visibly include the chemistry layer, not treat it as an implied application. The branch needs to show that thermodynamics governs reactions and compositions just as much as it governs generic state changes.

## Key Properties

- Works with macroscopic variables such as temperature, pressure, volume, energy, and entropy
- Explains equilibrium, spontaneity, and limits on energy conversion
- Includes a major chemistry-facing layer through free energy, chemical potential, and reaction equilibrium
- Applies across chemistry, physics, engineering, combustion, and materials science
- Supports reasoning about phase behavior, reaction direction, and system constraints
- Often paired with kinetics, which asks how fast a process occurs rather than whether it is favored

## Relationships

- **Is-a:** Foundational physical-science framework
- **Has-part:** State variables, equilibrium, spontaneous processes, energy balances, entropy analysis, free-energy reasoning
- **Related:** [[Entropy]], [[Chemical Thermodynamics]], [[Gibbs Free Energy]], [[Chemical Potential]], [[Materials Science]], [[Phase Diagram]], [[Carbon Cycle]], [[Carbon Capture and Storage]]
- **Used-by:** Chemical thermodynamics, combustion science, materials thermodynamics, energy systems, statistical and computational extensions

## Examples

- Determining whether a chemical reaction is favored under specified temperature and pressure conditions
- Explaining why heat flows spontaneously from hotter bodies to colder ones
- Using equilibrium reasoning to understand combustion products or phase stability in a material system
