# Phase Diagram vs Phase Transition

[[Phase Diagram]] and [[Phase Transition]] are closely connected, but they are not the same thing. A phase diagram is a representation of stability regions and boundaries. A phase transition is the actual transformation process that occurs when a system moves across those conditions.

## Comparison Table

| Dimension | [[Phase Diagram]] | [[Phase Transition]] |
|---|---|---|
| **What it is** | Representation of phase stability across changing conditions | Actual transformation from one phase to another |
| **Primary role** | Shows where phases are expected to be stable | Describes what happens when conditions drive a system across stability boundaries |
| **Typical variables** | Temperature, pressure, composition | Time, pathway, nucleation, growth, and changing conditions |
| **Thermodynamic role** | Encodes equilibrium structure | Expresses the process of moving between stable or metastable states |
| **Kinetic role** | Usually does not show pathway speed directly | Often strongly shaped by [[Kinetics]] and transport limits |
| **Use in materials science** | Planning and interpreting stable regions for materials systems | Explaining real structural change during heating, cooling, or processing |

## Phase Diagram as a Stability Map

[[Phase Diagram]] is best understood as a map. It tells you which phases are favored under particular conditions and where boundaries between those regions lie. This is why it is so useful in materials design, alloy analysis, and thermodynamics teaching. It condenses equilibrium information into a form that can be read visually.

But a map is not the same thing as movement across the map. A phase diagram may tell you where a new phase becomes favored without telling you how quickly the system reaches it or what intermediate structures appear on the way.

## Phase Transition as the Process Itself

[[Phase Transition]] refers to the actual change in state or structure. It is the event or process that occurs when conditions shift enough for a different phase to appear or become preferred. In real systems, this process often depends on nucleation, growth, diffusion, and other kinetic effects.

This is why [[Kinetics]] matters so much here. The phase diagram may tell you that a transition is expected, but the observed transformation can still depend strongly on pathway and timescale.

## Why the Distinction Matters

For GraphWiki, separating the two makes the materials and thermodynamics branches easier to understand. The diagram is a tool for representing equilibrium structure. The transition is a physical process. One supports interpretation; the other explains change.
