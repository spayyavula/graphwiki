# Thermodynamics vs Kinetics

[[Thermodynamics]] and [[Kinetics]] are often taught together because they answer complementary questions about the same process. Thermodynamics asks whether change is favored and what equilibrium state is expected. Kinetics asks how fast change occurs and what pathway controls it.

## Comparison Table

| Dimension | [[Thermodynamics]] | [[Kinetics]] |
|---|---|---|
| **Main question** | Is a process favored and what state is stable? | How fast does the process occur and by what mechanism? |
| **Primary focus** | Energy, entropy, equilibrium, state functions | Rates, mechanisms, activation barriers, transport limits |
| **Typical output** | Equilibrium direction, stability criteria, energy balances | Rate law, timescale, pathway, mechanistic control |
| **What it does not tell you** | Whether a favorable process will happen quickly | Whether the final state is the most stable one |
| **Common role in materials** | Defines phase stability landscape | Determines how microstructure actually develops |
| **Practical implication** | Says what is possible or favored | Says whether it is reachable on useful timescales |

## Thermodynamics: Favorability and Equilibrium

[[Thermodynamics]] provides the constraint framework for physical and chemical change. It explains the role of energy conservation, entropy, and equilibrium, and it tells you which states are favored under specified conditions. A phase predicted as stable on a [[Phase Diagram]] is a thermodynamic statement.

What thermodynamics does not tell you by itself is whether the system will reach that state quickly. That is why many metastable states can persist for long periods even when they are not the lowest-energy arrangement available.

## Kinetics: Pathway and Timescale

[[Kinetics]] addresses the missing part of the story. It asks what barriers, transport limitations, or mechanisms slow or accelerate a transformation. In reactions and phase changes, kinetics explains why equilibrium may be approached slowly, incompletely, or along a pathway that produces intermediate structures.

This matters especially in materials processing, where cooling rate, diffusion, and nucleation behavior often determine what structure is actually observed. In real systems, kinetic limitations can be just as important as thermodynamic favorability.

## Why Both Are Needed

The two frameworks are not rivals. They are complementary. Thermodynamics defines the destination landscape; kinetics constrains the route and the time required to get there. In GraphWiki, keeping them distinct makes it easier to explain equilibrium, phase behavior, and processing outcomes without collapsing all process reasoning into one vague idea of “change.”
