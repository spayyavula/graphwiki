# Carbon Cycle vs Carbon Capture and Storage

[[Carbon Cycle]] and [[Carbon Capture and Storage]] are tightly connected, but they sit at different levels of analysis. The carbon cycle is a natural earth-system process; carbon capture and storage is an engineered intervention designed to alter how carbon moves through that system.

## Comparison Table

| Dimension | [[Carbon Cycle]] | [[Carbon Capture and Storage]] |
|---|---|---|
| **What it is** | Natural biogeochemical system of carbon reservoirs and fluxes | Engineering and infrastructure approach for capturing and storing CO2 |
| **Primary focus** | Understanding sources, sinks, reservoirs, and feedbacks | Preventing emissions from reaching the atmosphere or storing them long term |
| **Timescale** | Days to millions of years | Operational and project timescales, from facilities to long-term storage monitoring |
| **Core actors** | Atmosphere, ocean, soils, vegetation, geologic reservoirs | Industrial plants, transport systems, storage sites, regulators, monitoring systems |
| **Scientific question** | How carbon moves and accumulates across Earth systems | Whether CO2 can be captured, transported, and stored at useful scale and acceptable risk |
| **Policy role** | Explains climate context and emissions imbalance | One candidate mitigation tool within decarbonization portfolios |
| **Graph/KG implication** | Reservoir-and-flux graph with feedback relationships | Process-and-infrastructure graph with facilities, pipelines, sites, and monitoring links |

## Carbon Cycle as System Description

[[Carbon Cycle]] is the broader explanatory framework. It describes how carbon moves among the atmosphere, biosphere, oceans, soils, and geologic storage over multiple timescales. It is the core concept for understanding why atmospheric CO2 accumulates, why sinks matter, and how human emissions perturb a preexisting earth system.

Because it is fundamentally a systems concept, the carbon cycle is a natural fit for graph modeling. Reservoirs, processes, and fluxes can be treated as connected entities rather than isolated facts. This is why it functions as the organizing concept for the current Carbon Science branch.

## Carbon Capture and Storage as Intervention

[[Carbon Capture and Storage]] starts from a different question: given the scale of anthropogenic emissions, can some fraction of CO2 be redirected away from the atmosphere and placed into long-term storage? CCS therefore depends on the carbon cycle for its scientific relevance, but it is not itself a description of the natural system. It is an attempt to change the path carbon takes through that system.

That makes CCS more infrastructure-heavy and decision-heavy. Pipelines, injection sites, monitoring regimes, leakage risk, and cost all matter. In graph terms, the focus shifts from earth-system reservoirs and fluxes to industrial assets, storage pathways, and intervention outcomes.

## Why the Distinction Matters

The two ideas are often discussed together, but treating them as the same topic weakens both. The carbon cycle tells you why atmospheric accumulation is happening and where carbon can move. CCS asks whether a specific engineered pathway can redirect some of that carbon safely and at scale. One is descriptive and explanatory; the other is operational and mitigative.

For GraphWiki, keeping the distinction explicit makes the Carbon Science branch easier to expand. A future carbon-cycle graph can center on reservoirs, sinks, and feedbacks, while a future CCS graph can center on capture methods, transport, storage, and verification.
