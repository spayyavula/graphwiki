# Carbon Science Academic Source Guide

**Definition:** A starter guide to the academic source base for building the Carbon Science branch of GraphWiki.

## Overview
[coverage: medium]

Carbon science is a broad label. It can mean the global carbon cycle and climate system, carbon management and sequestration, or the chemistry and physics of carbon-based materials. For GraphWiki, this first source guide deliberately spans those three branches so the topic can be developed honestly before it is narrowed into a more specialized dataset.

This guide highlights a first university-hosted source base anchored in carbon-cycle science, climate-linked carbon flows, carbon capture, and carbon materials. It is enough to support a credible starter topic path, but not yet enough to treat Carbon Science as a finished first-class graph dataset. The branch is now strengthened by a Harry Marsh book-level introduction to carbon science, a requested fullerene literature thread, carbon-carbon composite reviews centered on aerospace and aircraft-brake applications, explicit carbon-catalysis coverage, a graphene-semiconductor thread, clearer support for activated-carbon and catalyst-support reasoning, and new branch notes on coal, carbon in energy systems, carbon's biology-facing role through DNA, and the discovery history of DNA structure.

## Recommended Reading Order
[coverage: medium]

1. Start with the UCAR carbon-cycle science plan and the Rutgers global carbon-cycle paper for the big-picture scientific frame.
2. Use the Hawaii and Washington lecture materials for more accessible, teaching-oriented views of fluxes, reservoirs, and climate interaction.
3. Read the Michigan State forest-carbon material for land-management and sink/source framing.
4. Use the Berkeley carbon-capture source to extend the topic into mitigation and storage.
5. Use the Harry Marsh carbon-science material to widen the branch from climate carbon into the broader structure, processing, and materials-science view of carbon.
6. Use the fullerene note and the Purdue allotrope material to make the carbon-materials branch more complete rather than leaving fullerenes implicit.
7. Use the coal-and-energy note to make the industrial energy branch explicit rather than leaving fossil carbon implicit.
8. Use the carbon-in-biology note to show that carbon also belongs in biomolecular structure, with DNA as the clearest anchor example.
9. Use the DNA-discovery history note if you want a small history-of-science extension that explains why DNA structure mattered.
10. Finish with the carbon-carbon composite review material to connect carbon materials to aircraft brakes, thermo-structural design, and aerospace applications.
11. Use the carbon-catalysis reviews to extend the branch from carbon materials into electrocatalysis, oxygen reduction, and CO2 conversion.
12. Use the graphene-semiconductor note to connect the carbon-materials branch to electronic materials and semiconductor-device thinking.
13. Use the carbon-catalysis review again when distinguishing activated carbon, carbon as support, and genuinely active carbon frameworks.

## Source Clusters
[coverage: medium]

- **Carbon cycle foundations:** UCAR, Rutgers, Hawaii, Washington
- **Land and management context:** Michigan State forest-carbon source
- **Carbon capture and storage:** Berkeley CCS source
- **Carbon in energy systems:** Coal formation, use, emissions, and carbon-management context
- **Carbon materials:** MIT nanotubes review, Purdue allotropes material, Harry Marsh's carbon-science overview, fullerene literature thread
- **Carbon in biology:** DNA as a carbon-based biomolecular structure and biology-facing extension point
- **DNA discovery history:** Double-helix discovery and the link between molecular structure and biological information
- **Carbon composites and applications:** Carbon-carbon composite overview and aircraft-brake literature
- **Carbon catalysis:** Carbon catalyst reviews on oxygen reduction, CO2 conversion, active-site design, and doped/defect-engineered carbon frameworks
- **Carbon electronics and semiconductors:** Graphene as a semiconductor-facing material, including heterostructures and band-gap-engineering discussions
- **Surface area and support roles:** Activated carbon, adsorption-heavy carbon materials, and the support-vs-true-catalyst distinction

## How To Use This Guide In GraphWiki
[coverage: medium]

- Use it to decide whether the eventual topic graph should center on climate-carbon systems or on carbon materials and nanoscience.
- Use the carbon-cycle sources for future concept pages on fluxes, reservoirs, sinks, and anthropogenic perturbation.
- Use the coal-and-energy note when extending the topic toward industrial carbon, fuel use, combustion, and energy-system decarbonization.
- Use the MIT and Purdue sources if GraphWiki later adds a materials-heavy carbon branch with graphene, nanotubes, and allotropes.
- Use the DNA note when extending the topic toward biomolecular carbon without turning Carbon Science into a full genomics branch.
- Use the DNA-discovery note when a small history-of-science layer would clarify why DNA structure became foundational.
- Use the Marsh and fullerene notes to justify treating carbon materials as a real branch rather than a single `Carbon Allotrope` placeholder.
- Use the carbon-carbon composite literature when extending the topic toward friction materials, aircraft brakes, and thermo-structural aerospace applications.
- Use the carbon-catalysis reviews when extending the topic toward active-site design, electrocatalysis, green chemistry, and CO2-conversion pathways.
- Use the graphene-semiconductor note when extending the topic toward electronic materials, device interfaces, optoelectronics, and band-gap engineering.
- Use the same catalysis review thread when separating carbon's role as a support from carbon's role as an active catalytic medium.
