# Materials Science Academic Source Guide

**Definition:** A starter guide to the academic source base for building the Materials Science branch of GraphWiki.

## Overview
[coverage: medium]

Materials science combines structure, chemistry, mechanics, phase behavior, and thermodynamics to explain why materials behave the way they do. For GraphWiki, the most useful starter source set needs to cover both the structural side of the field and the energetic side, especially phase transitions and thermodynamics of materials. It also needs to leave room for the physics-facing angle of the subject, where structure, vibrations, elasticity, and transformations are treated as central explanatory objects.

This guide highlights a first university-hosted source base centered on MIT, Berkeley, and Caltech. These sources are especially useful for building a topic graph that connects materials structure, solid-state chemistry, phase diagrams, vibrational thermodynamics, materials thermodynamics, and the mechanics-facing side of the field.

## Recommended Reading Order
[coverage: medium]

1. Start with MIT Fundamentals of Materials Science and Structure of Materials.
2. Use MIT solid-state chemistry and thermodynamics-of-materials material to connect structure to energetics.
3. Read the Berkeley materials, thermodynamics, and continuum-mechanics material to shape the mechanics and physics-facing side of the branch.
4. Use the Caltech review and phase-transition sources for advanced material behavior, vibrational reasoning, and transformations.

## Source Clusters
[coverage: medium]

- **Introductory foundations:** MIT 3.012, MIT 3.012sx
- **Structure and chemistry:** MIT solid-state chemistry, Berkeley continuum mechanics context
- **Thermodynamics of materials:** MIT 3.020, Berkeley E40 syllabus, Caltech vibrational thermodynamics review
- **Phase behavior:** Caltech phase diagrams and phase transitions sources
- **Physics-facing angle:** Structure of materials, vibrational thermodynamics, phase transitions, and continuum behavior
