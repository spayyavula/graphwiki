# Carbon catalysis review note

- **Primary source 1:** Xia et al., *Carbon catalysts for CO2 conversion: From carbon emissions to zero-carbon solutions* (Science Advances, 2025)
- **DOI:** 10.1126/sciadv.ady9164
- **Primary source 2:** Wei et al., *PGM-free carbon-based catalysts for the electrocatalytic oxygen reduction reaction: active sites and activity enhancement* (Energy Materials, 2023)
- **DOI:** 10.20517/energymater.2023.52
- **Why it matters for GraphWiki:** These reviews show that carbon is not only a support, filler, or structural material. Carbon can function as an effective catalyst family in its own right, especially when activity is created through heteroatom doping, defect engineering, adsorption-induced charge transfer, porosity control, and metal-carbon interfacial design.
- **Key extracted points:**
  - Carbon catalysts can be designed by breaking electronic symmetry in graphitic networks and creating catalytically active centers through heteroatom doping, defect engineering, molecular adsorption, and architecture control.
  - Nitrogen-doped CNTs, graphene-derived systems, porous carbons, and related carbon nanostructures can act as metal-free or low-critical-mineral electrocatalysts.
  - Carbon catalysts are prominent in oxygen reduction reaction research and in CO2 conversion, where they can steer selectivity toward CO, multicarbon products, urea, and other value-added chemicals.
  - Carbon materials can contribute catalytically in several roles: as metal-free catalysts, as doped catalytic frameworks, and as carbon-supported hybrid catalysts whose interfacial effects change activity and selectivity.
  - Fullerenes are relevant to this branch because the Science Advances review explicitly treats them as electron acceptors in adsorption-induced charge-transfer schemes for carbon catalyst design.
- **Useful examples surfaced in the sources:**
  - Nitrogen-doped CNT arrays functioning as metal-free catalysts for oxygen reduction.
  - N-doped graphene quantum dots and ordered mesoporous N-doped carbons enabling selective CO2-to-C2+ chemistry.
  - Carbon-supported Cu, Fe, and Co systems where metal-carbon interactions alter catalytic pathways.
